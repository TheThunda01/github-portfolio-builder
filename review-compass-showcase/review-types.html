Review type guide

Start by asking what the review is supposed to do.

A review label should follow the purpose of the project. This page helps students match a goal to a review type before they commit to a search strategy or extraction form.

Quick fit

Purpose, frame, output, and caution.

Systematic review

Best for: Answering a focused question with explicit eligibility criteria.

Frame: PICO.

Watch-out: Do not use it for a broad map if the question is not yet focused.

Meta-analysis

Best for: Pooling comparable quantitative outcomes across similar studies.

Frame: PICO with a pre-planned effect-size approach.

Watch-out: Numbers alone are not enough; outcomes and designs must be compatible.

Scoping review

Best for: Mapping available evidence, key concepts, and knowledge gaps.

Frame: PCC, which stands for Population, Concept, and Context.

Watch-out: Do not present it as a guideline-making review unless appraisal supports that claim.

Evidence map

Best for: Showing evidence clusters across categories, populations, or settings.

Frame: PCC plus stable charting categories.

Watch-out: Pilot categories before making the map look final.

Rapid review

Best for: Time-sensitive decisions when a full review is not feasible.

Frame: Usually PICO, with documented shortcuts.

Watch-out: Every restriction or omission needs a rationale and limitation statement.

Umbrella review

Best for: Comparing conclusions from existing systematic reviews or meta-analyses.

Frame: PICOS or another review-level eligibility frame.

Watch-out: Keep review-level evidence separate from primary-study evidence.

Qualitative evidence synthesis

Best for: Synthesizing experiences, perceptions, barriers, facilitators, or meanings.

Frame: SPIDER or a similar qualitative question frame.

Watch-out: Keep participant voice, author interpretation, and reviewer interpretation distinct.

Mixed-methods review

Best for: Integrating outcomes with qualitative explanations.

Frame: PICO, PCC, or a planned combination.

Watch-out: Plan integration early so one evidence type does not quietly dominate.

Integrative review

Best for: Combining empirical, theoretical, or practice-based sources.

Frame: PCC or a topic map with clear source types.

Watch-out: Define the boundary so the review does not become arbitrary.

Narrative review

Best for: Explaining a broad topic, history, or theory.

Frame: Topic, population, context, and themes.

Watch-out: Be honest that this is not a systematic search unless systematic methods were used.

Realist review

Best for: Explaining how and why a program works in particular contexts.

Frame: Context, Mechanism, and Outcome.

Watch-out: It needs theory refinement, not only a list of study results.

Question frames

Use a frame because it makes the hidden decisions visible.

Frame Useful when Student-friendly prompt
PICO Focused intervention, exposure, comparison, or outcome questions. Among this population, how is this intervention or exposure related to these outcomes?
PCC Scoping, mapping, and broad exploratory questions. What is known about this concept for this population in this context?
SPIDER Qualitative questions about experience, meaning, and perception. How does this sample describe or experience this phenomenon?
CMO Realist questions about programs, context, and mechanisms. In this context, what mechanism may explain this outcome?

Rationale

The app asks for review type before search terms for a reason.

Cochrane explains that the review question guides eligibility criteria, searching, data collection, synthesis, and presentation. JBI guidance for scoping reviews similarly ties the title, question, and inclusion criteria to Population, Concept, and Context. Review Compass reflects that logic by asking students to clarify the purpose before generating search and extraction drafts.
