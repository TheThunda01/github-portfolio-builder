Cochrane-informed process

A thorough review plan is built in layers.

The Cochrane Handbook is written for systematic reviews of interventions, but many of its planning habits are useful for student evidence syntheses: state the question, define eligibility, document the search, select studies transparently, collect data consistently, appraise bias, synthesize carefully, and report the certainty and limitations.

1 Scope and question

Clarify whether the project is broad, narrow, or a staged review.

2 Eligibility

Pre-specify population, intervention or concept, comparator, outcomes, and study types.

3 Search and selection

Use multiple appropriate sources, record exact strategies, and document record flow.

4 Data collection

Use a structured, piloted form that is tied to the question and criteria.

5 Bias and synthesis

Match appraisal and analysis choices to the study designs and available data.

6 Reporting

Use the right checklist and explain certainty, limitations, and conflicts.

Step 1

Define the question and scope.

Cochrane describes the review question as the first major decision because it guides eligibility, searching, data collection, synthesis, and presentation. For student projects, this means the topic should become a complete question before the search is treated as final.

  • Write the primary objective in one complete sentence.
  • Decide whether the question is focused enough for PICO or broader and better suited to PCC.
  • Check for overlapping reviews and ongoing protocols before investing weeks of work.
  • Ask who will use the review and what decision, map, or learning goal it should support.

Step 2

Pre-specify eligibility criteria and grouping decisions.

Cochrane distinguishes systematic reviews from narrative reviews by the use of pre-specified inclusion and exclusion criteria. Those criteria should be connected to the question and to the planned synthesis, not invented after seeing the search results.

  • Define who or what must be studied, what concept or intervention must appear, and which study designs are eligible.
  • State what will be excluded and why, especially protocols without results, opinion pieces, wrong populations, or wrong settings.
  • Plan how related populations, interventions, outcomes, or study designs will be grouped for synthesis.
  • Document and justify any later eligibility changes instead of quietly changing the rules.

Step 3

Build a reproducible search with an information specialist when possible.

Cochrane recommends a systematic and comprehensive approach to identifying eligible studies and strongly recommends peer review of search strategies by a suitably qualified librarian or information specialist. PRISMA-S adds a reporting checklist for sources, search strategies, limits, dates, and deduplication.

  • Name every database, platform, registry, website, and supplementary source.
  • Keep the full search strategy for each source, not just a list of concepts.
  • Record the date searched, limits used, number of records retrieved, and deduplication process.
  • Avoid arbitrary date and language restrictions unless the restriction is justified and reported.

Step 4

Use a structured data collection form and pilot it.

Cochrane states that systematic reviews should collect data with structured forms and that piloting the form is highly desirable. Method research also finds frequent guidance recommending customized or adapted forms, clear instructions, training, and pilot testing.

  • Define each field before extraction begins.
  • Pilot the form on a small sample of records and revise unclear fields.
  • Keep citation details, design, population, intervention or concept, outcomes, findings, and limitations traceable.
  • Document disagreements and how the team resolved them.

Step 5

Assess bias with the right tool for the included evidence.

Cochrane reviews include risk-of-bias assessment. For randomized trials, Cochrane recommends RoB 2, which uses domain-level signalling questions and written justifications. Other designs require other fit-for-purpose appraisal tools.

  • Choose the appraisal tool before extraction when possible.
  • Assess the result or outcome that matters for the review question, not only the article as a whole.
  • Write a reason for each judgment so readers can see how the conclusion was reached.
  • Reflect bias concerns in synthesis and limitations instead of hiding them in a table.

Step 6

Plan synthesis before collecting extra data.

Meta-analysis can be appropriate when studies are sufficiently comparable and effect estimates can be combined responsibly. Cochrane also reminds authors to consider heterogeneity, missing data, and whether a summary effect is interpretable. If pooling is not appropriate, a structured narrative, map, or thematic synthesis may be more honest.

  • State whether findings will be grouped by population, concept, intervention, outcome, context, or design.
  • Only pool data when the comparison, outcome, and study designs are compatible enough to support it.
  • Track heterogeneity and explain why results do or do not belong together.
  • Use GRADE for important intervention outcomes when the review is assessing certainty of effects.

Rapid review note

Shortcuts are methods decisions, not hidden conveniences.

The Cochrane Rapid Reviews Methods Group describes rapid reviews as using systematic and explicit methods while narrowing, restricting, or omitting parts of the full systematic review process to answer urgent questions more quickly. A student rapid review should therefore state exactly what was shortened, why that shortcut was acceptable, and how it may affect confidence.
